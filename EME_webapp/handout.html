CPE 101 - Introduction to Computer Engineering

Embedded Sytems

An embedded system is a specialized computing system that combines hardware and software to perform a specific function or set of functions within a larger mechanical or electrical system. Unlike general-purpose computers, such as desktops or laptops, which are designed to handle a wide range of tasks, embedded systems are dedicated to particular applications. They are often integrated into devices that we use in our daily lives, including washing machines, mobile phones, automobiles, smart watches, and medical devices.


Embedded systems are characterized by their reliability, compactness, efficiency, and real-time operation. They are built to perform their assigned tasks accurately and consistently, often under strict time constraints. A typical embedded system operates continuously for long periods, which makes stability and durability essential qualities. These systems are also designed to be resource-efficient, as they usually have limited processing power, memory, and storage capacity compared to general-purpose systems.


An embedded system generally consists of two main components: hardware and software. The hardware component includes a microcontroller or microprocessor, which serves as the brain of the system and executes instructions. It also includes memory units such as ROM (for program storage) and RAM (for temporary data), along with input and output devices like sensors, actuators, and displays. The software component, often referred to as firmware, provides the instructions that control the hardware. In more complex systems, a Real-Time Operating System (RTOS) may be used to manage tasks and ensure timely responses to inputs.
